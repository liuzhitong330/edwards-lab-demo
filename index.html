VMAT2, α-Synuclein, GBA, and the Dopamine Corelease Circuit — A Regional Expression Map

The Edwards lab identified the vesicular monoamine transporters and has built a mechanistic framework for how vesicular filling, neurotransmitter corelease, and presynaptic protein function — particularly α-synuclein's role in promoting fusion pore dilation — go wrong in Parkinson's disease. Two threads from recent lab work I find especially compelling: (1) the finding that dopamine neurons in the VTA but not the SNc preferentially coreleaase glutamate via VGLUT2, and (2) the mechanistic connection between glucocerebrosidase (GBA) and α-synuclein, which the lab has been investigating in ongoing work on GBA–synuclein interactions in PD.

I pulled in situ hybridization expression data from the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas for eleven presynaptic and PD-relevant genes across eight brain regions — spanning the dopaminergic circuit (SNc, VTA, striatum, nucleus accumbens) and reference regions — to map where these proteins are co-expressed and what that pattern implies for PD vulnerability and the corelease question.

What this is. Personal exploratory analysis, not novel research. All data from the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas (Allen Institute for Brain Science, 2004), accessed via the public RMA API. ISH expression energy is a bulk regional metric. Analysis and interpretation are mine alone.

What the data shows

GBA (Gba) and TH (Th) co-peak in substantia nigra. Gba shows highest expression in the substantia nigra (0.057, co-localized with Th at 2.32) — placing glucocerebrosidase at highest concentration in the very dopamine neurons that degenerate in PD. GBA loss-of-function is the most common genetic PD risk factor, and the co-distribution of GBA and TH in SNc makes sense of that epidemiology: GBA dysfunction would impair lysosomal clearance of α-synuclein specifically in the neurons where it is most needed. The lab's current focus on the GBA–synuclein interaction in PD pathogenesis is directly legible in this expression pattern.
VGLUT2 (Slc17a6) is enriched in VTA relative to SN (6.43 vs 1.21). This asymmetry captures the corelease phenomenon the Edwards lab has studied: VTA dopamine neurons preferentially express VGLUT2 and coreleaase glutamate alongside dopamine, while SNc neurons are more purely dopaminergic. The co-expression of VMAT2 (Slc18a2) with VGLUT2 in VTA — and the synergistic vesicle-filling mechanism the lab has characterized — would thus be particularly active in VTA circuits, with distinct implications for the two dopaminergic projections (mesolimbic vs nigrostriatal) in terms of both physiology and PD vulnerability.
  • VMAT2 (Slc18a2) peaks in dorsal raphe (8.72), the serotonergic nucleus — consistent with VMAT2's role as the shared vesicular transporter for all monoamines. The original selection of VMAT2 using MPP+ resistance established dopaminergic neurodegeneration as a vesicular transport problem; the high raphe expression is a reminder that the same vulnerability axis applies to the serotonergic system.
  • γ-synuclein (Sncg) peaks in substantia nigra (14.28) — the highest expression of any synuclein family member in that region. α-synuclein (Snca) is broadly expressed at low levels, consistent with its ubiquitous presynaptic role. The SN enrichment of γ-synuclein raises the question of whether it interacts with or competes with α-synuclein at dopaminergic terminals — a presynaptic competition that could modulate PD pathogenesis.
  • DAT (Slc6a3) is highest in VTA (2.76), consistent with the mesolimbic system's dense dopamine reuptake machinery. DAT levels determine cytosolic dopamine exposure — high DAT activity both recaptures dopamine from the synapse and produces cytosolic dopamine if VMAT2 is overwhelmed, directly relevant to oxidative stress models of SN degeneration.

Why this connects to the lab's work

  • The Edwards lab's current work on GBA and α-synuclein in PD centers on understanding how these two proteins interact at the presynaptic terminal and in lysosomes. The regional co-expression map I've built here places that interaction most prominently in the substantia nigra — which is precisely where PD pathology begins. I would like to contribute to the experimental side of this question, both in terms of biochemical assays and in terms of computational analysis of the datasets that follow from them.
  • The corelease story is one I find particularly interesting from a mechanistic standpoint: the synergistic vesicle-filling between VMAT2 and VGLUT2, and the distinct vesicle populations that recycle through different mechanisms, raises questions about how α-synuclein's influence on pore dilation interacts with the co-release machinery specifically in VTA vs SNc neurons.
  • My background combines molecular and cell biology wet lab skills with computational data analysis. I think my combination of practical lab experience and computational background is where I can be most useful to your lab.

Methods
